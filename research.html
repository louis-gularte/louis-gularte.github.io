Much of philosophical work is focused on negative emotion(s) and suffering in particular – including what it is, why it matters (if it does), what to do about it, and even what role it plays in our sense of being an agent or 'having a self.'

My cognitive science MSc thesis (which I'm currently finishing up in Michael Frank's Laboratory for Neural Computation and Cognition) explores the relationship between negative and positive emotion ('affective valence') and the reward and punishment system, best known for its role in reinforcement learning.

Papers in preparation

Suffering and attention

This paper gives an account of negative affect and an account of suffering and uses it to explain a puzzling pattern of cases in which allowing ourselves to suffer more thoroughly radically transforms our suffering for the better. The three cases of 'better-because-worse' suffering I consider are ① 'acceptance-stage' grief, ② deeply moving aesthetic experiences that make us feel extreme sadness or even despair, and ③ experiences accompanying meditation practices that involve focused attention on our suffering.
